example of divided difference interpolaton


To illustrate how one interpolates a function using divided differences, we will interpolate sin⁡40∘ from the sines of 0∘, 30∘, 45∘, 60∘, and 90∘. To keep from having too many zeros in our numbers, we will actually interpolate sin⁡(10⁢x) instead.

We begin by making a divided difference table:

0.00.00000.16673.00.500-0.006360.1381-0.0017864.50.7071-0.01071-0.00014450.1060-0.0004866.00.8660-0.013620.04479.01.0000

Reading off the top numbers from each column, we may form the following divided difference series:

sin⁡(10⁢x)=0.1667⁢x-0.00636⁢x⁢(x-3) -0.001786⁢x⁢(x-3)⁢(x-4.5)
-0.0001445⁢x⁢(x-3)⁢(x-4.5)⁢(x-6)+R

Substituting 0.4 for x, we obtain 0.6502 as an approximate value for sin⁡40∘. When compared with the actual value of 0.6428, this is a reasonable approximation —it is correct to 1%.
